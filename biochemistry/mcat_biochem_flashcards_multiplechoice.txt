## PROTEIN CHEMISTRY & AMINO ACIDS

At pH lower than a protein's pI, what net charge does the protein carry? (A) Positive (B) Negative (C) Neutral (D) Variable	A - Below pI → protein gains protons → net positive charge. Rule: pH < pI = positive

At pH higher than a protein's pI, what net charge does the protein carry? (A) Positive (B) Negative (C) Neutral (D) Variable	B - Above pI → protein loses protons → net negative charge. Rule: pH > pI = negative

What happens to acidic amino acid side chains (Asp, Glu) at low pH? (A) Become deprotonated and negative (B) Become protonated and neutral (C) Remain unchanged (D) Form disulfide bonds	B - Low pH → protonation of -COO⁻ → -COOH (neutral). Rule: acidic residues neutralized at low pH

What happens to basic amino acid side chains (Lys, Arg, His) at low pH? (A) Become deprotonated and neutral (B) Become protonated and positively charged (C) Remain neutral (D) Form salt bridges	B - Low pH → protonation of amine groups → NH₃⁺ (positive). Rule: basic residues charged at low pH

Why does changing pH alter protein solubility? (A) Breaks peptide bonds (B) Changes hydrophobicity (C) Changing net charge alters electrostatic repulsion (D) Denatures all proteins	C - Charge changes affect protein-protein interactions; isoelectric point → minimum solubility. Rule: charge → repulsion → solubility

In ion-exchange chromatography, what determines binding strength? (A) Size (B) Hydrophobicity (C) Net charge relative to column type (D) Molecular weight	C - Cation exchange binds (+); anion exchange binds (−); strength depends on charge density. Rule: charge match determines binding

Which amino acid contains a primary amine used for Schiff base formation? (A) Histidine (B) Lysine (C) Arginine (D) Asparagine	B - Lysine ε-amino group forms Schiff bases (imines) with aldehydes/ketones. Rule: Lys = nucleophilic amine

Which amino acid category is involved in catalysis, nucleophilic attack, and Schiff base chemistry? (A) Hydrophobic (B) Aromatic (C) Basic charged residues (especially Lysine) (D) Acidic charged	C - Lys, His, Arg provide nucleophilic/basic groups for catalysis. Rule: basic = catalytic

Which amino acid acts as a proton shuttle near physiologic pH? (A) Lysine (B) Arginine (C) Histidine (D) Aspartate	C - His pKa ~6 → can accept/donate protons at pH 7. Rule: His = pH 7 buffer

Which amino acid has a guanidinium group for strong ionic interactions? (A) Lysine (B) Arginine (C) Histidine (D) Glutamate	B - Arg guanidinium is positively charged, forms strong salt bridges. Rule: Arg = strongest positive charge

Which amino acids are acidic and negatively charged? (A) Lys, Arg (B) Aspartate, Glutamate (C) Ser, Thr (D) Phe, Tyr	B - Asp and Glu have carboxyl groups (−COO⁻) at pH 7. Rule: D and E = acidic

Which amino acids have OH groups used for phosphorylation? (A) Cys, Met (B) Asn, Gln (C) Serine, Threonine, Tyrosine (D) Leu, Ile, Val	C - Ser, Thr, Tyr hydroxyl groups are phosphorylation targets. Rule: STY = phosphorylation sites

Which amino acid's thiol forms disulfide bonds? (A) Methionine (B) Serine (C) Cysteine (D) Threonine	C - Cys -SH groups oxidize to form Cys-S-S-Cys. Rule: Cys = disulfide bonds

Which amino acids contain amide side chains? (A) Asp, Glu (B) Asparagine, Glutamine (C) Lys, Arg (D) Ser, Thr	B - Asn and Gln have -CONH₂ groups. Rule: N and Q = amides

Which amino acids are aromatic? (A) Leu, Ile, Val (B) Phenylalanine, Tyrosine, Tryptophan (C) Ser, Thr, Cys (D) Asp, Glu, Asn	B - Phe, Tyr, Trp have aromatic rings. Rule: F, Y, W = aromatic

Which two amino acids are structural modifiers? (A) Leu, Ile (B) Asp, Glu (C) Proline (kink), Glycine (flexibility) (D) Ser, Thr	C - Pro introduces kinks; Gly allows flexibility. Rule: P = rigid kink, G = flexible

Which amino acids drive membrane insertion and hydrophobic collapse? (A) Asp, Glu, Lys (B) Ser, Thr, Asn (C) Leu, Ile, Val, Phe, Met, Trp, Ala (D) His, Arg, Gln	C - Hydrophobic residues partition into lipid bilayers and protein cores. Rule: LIVFMWA = hydrophobic core

What amino acid category forms catalytic sites and drug binding regions? (A) Hydrophobic (B) Charged amino acids (C) Aromatic only (D) Small amino acids	B - Charged residues (D, E, K, R, H) in active sites for catalysis and ionic drug interactions. Rule: charged = active sites

Mutation of charged residues usually disrupts what? (A) Membrane insertion (B) Catalysis or protein folding (C) Disulfide bonds (D) Phosphorylation	B - Charge changes affect electrostatic interactions critical for structure/function. Rule: charge mutation = function loss

Which amino acids are strongly hydrophobic? (A) Ser, Thr, Asn (B) Leu, Ile, Val, Phe (C) Lys, Arg, His (D) Asp, Glu, Gln	B - LIVF = most hydrophobic; partition into membranes and cores. Rule: branched/aromatic nonpolar = hydrophobic

Hydrophobic collapse failure leads to what pathology? (A) Scurvy (B) Sickle cell (C) Protein aggregation (Alzheimer's) (D) PKU	C - Misfolded hydrophobic regions exposed → aggregation (amyloid, prions). Rule: failed folding = aggregation

Which amino acids absorb UV light at 280 nm? (A) All amino acids (B) Tryptophan, Tyrosine, Phenylalanine (C) Charged amino acids (D) Hydrophobic amino acids	B - Aromatic rings absorb UV; used for protein concentration measurement. Rule: W, Y, (F) = 280 nm

Aromatic amino acids form precursors to what hormone family? (A) Steroids (B) Catecholamines (C) Insulin (D) Glucagon	B - Tyr → DOPA → dopamine, norepinephrine, epinephrine. Rule: Tyr = catecholamine precursor

Why do disulfide bonds matter clinically? (A) Energy storage (B) Stabilize extracellular proteins (insulin, antibodies) (C) Facilitate glycolysis (D) Transport oxygen	B - Oxidizing extracellular environment allows Cys-Cys bonds for stability. Rule: disulfides = extracellular stability

Which amino acid is precursor to GABA? (A) Glycine (B) Glutamate (C) Aspartate (D) Glutamine	B - Glutamate decarboxylase: Glu → GABA (inhibitory neurotransmitter). Rule: Glu → GABA

Mutation of proline often disrupts what? (A) Charge interactions (B) Disulfide bonds (C) Secondary structure (D) Phosphorylation	C - Pro kink disrupts α-helices; mutations alter structure. Rule: Pro mutation = structure disruption

Glycine is critical for what structural protein? (A) Hemoglobin (B) Insulin (C) Collagen (D) Actin	C - Every 3rd residue in collagen is Gly (allows tight triple helix). Rule: Gly = collagen flexibility

Scurvy affects hydroxylation of which amino acids? (A) Ser, Thr (B) Proline and lysine (C) Cys, Met (D) Asp, Glu	B - Vitamin C cofactor for prolyl/lysyl hydroxylases in collagen. Rule: scurvy = Pro/Lys hydroxylation

Why is vitamin C needed for collagen? (A) Cross-linking (B) Hydroxylation → stable triple helix (C) Glycosylation (D) Disulfide formation	B - Hydroxyproline and hydroxylysine stabilize collagen via H-bonds. Rule: Vit C = collagen stability

## PROTEIN STRUCTURE

Primary structure is defined by what bonds? (A) Hydrogen bonds (B) Peptide bonds (C) Disulfide bonds (D) Ionic bonds	B - Covalent peptide bonds link amino acids in sequence. Rule: 1° = peptide bonds

Secondary structure is stabilized by what? (A) Disulfide bonds (B) Hydrogen bonds (backbone) (C) Hydrophobic interactions (D) Ionic bonds	B - α-helix and β-sheet stabilized by backbone N-H···O=C H-bonds. Rule: 2° = backbone H-bonds (not side chains)

Tertiary structure is stabilized by which interactions? (A) Peptide bonds only (B) Hydrogen bonds only (C) Hydrophobic interactions, ionic bonds, disulfide bonds (D) Covalent bonds only	C - Side chain interactions fold the protein. Rule: 3° = side chain interactions

Quaternary structure involves what? (A) Single polypeptide folding (B) Assembly of multiple subunits (C) Peptide bond formation (D) Disulfide bonds only	B - Multiple polypeptides associate (e.g., hemoglobin tetramer). Rule: 4° = multi-subunit assembly

Heat shock proteins require what to refold proteins? (A) GTP (B) ATP (C) NAD⁺ (D) FAD	B - HSPs are ATP-dependent chaperones. Rule: HSP = ATP-dependent

High FRET indicates what? (A) Unfolded protein (B) Compact/misfolded conformation (C) Complete denaturation (D) No protein present	B - High FRET = close fluorophore proximity (compact). Rule: high FRET = compact

Low FRET indicates what? (A) Compact folding (B) Unfolded/refolding (C) Native state (D) Aggregation	B - Low FRET = distant fluorophores (extended). Rule: low FRET = unfolded

Misfolding exposes what regions? (A) Charged residues (B) Hydrophobic residues (C) Glycosylation sites (D) Disulfide bonds	B - Normally buried hydrophobic regions exposed → aggregation risk. Rule: misfolding = exposed hydrophobic

What degrades misfolded proteins? (A) Ribosomes (B) Proteasome (ubiquitin-tagged) (C) Golgi (D) Peroxisomes	B - Ubiquitin tags → 26S proteasome degrades. Rule: ubiquitin → proteasome

Why does denaturation not break peptide bonds? (A) Peptides are too strong (B) Peptide bond hydrolysis has high activation energy (C) No water present (D) GTP required	B - Peptide bonds thermodynamically unstable but kinetically stable (need proteases). Rule: denaturation = noncovalent only

Why are transmembrane helices enriched in hydrophobic amino acids? (A) Stronger structure (B) To minimize energetic cost in lipid bilayer (C) For signaling (D) Random distribution	B - Hydrophobic residues interact favorably with lipid tails. Rule: TM helix = hydrophobic

## ENZYME KINETICS

Km is substrate concentration at what point? (A) Vmax (B) 1/2 Vmax (C) 1/4 Vmax (D) 2× Vmax	B - Km = [S] when v = 1/2 Vmax. Rule: Km = half-saturation point

Low Km indicates what? (A) Low affinity (B) High affinity (C) Low Vmax (D) High Vmax	B - Low Km = high affinity (reaches 1/2 Vmax at low [S]). Rule: low Km = tight binding

Competitive inhibition changes what? (A) Vmax only (B) Km only (C) Km ↑, Vmax same (D) Both decrease	C - Inhibitor competes for active site; overcome with high [S]. Rule: competitive = Km↑ Vmax same

Noncompetitive inhibition changes what? (A) Km only (B) Vmax ↓, Km same (C) Both increase (D) Neither changes	B - Inhibitor binds allosteric site; reduces enzyme efficiency. Rule: noncompetitive = Vmax↓ Km same

Parallel lines on a Lineweaver-Burk plot indicate what type of inhibition? (A) Competitive (B) Noncompetitive (C) Uncompetitive (D) Mixed	C - Uncompetitive: same slope, different intercepts. Rule: parallel LB = uncompetitive

Suicide inhibitors are what? (A) Competitive reversible (B) Noncompetitive reversible (C) Irreversible inhibitors (D) Allosteric activators	C - Covalently modify active site permanently. Rule: suicide = irreversible

Clinical example of irreversible inhibition? (A) Aspirin on COX (B) Organophosphates inhibiting acetylcholinesterase (C) Statins on HMG-CoA reductase (D) Warfarin	B - Nerve agents covalently modify AChE serine. Rule: organophosphates = irreversible AChE inhibition

Catalysts lower what? (A) ΔG (B) Activation energy (Ea) (C) Product concentration (D) Substrate concentration	B - Enzymes lower Ea, not ΔG. Rule: catalyst = ↓Ea, ΔG unchanged

Do catalysts change ΔG? (A) Yes, decrease it (B) Yes, increase it (C) No (D) Only at high temperatures	C - Catalysts change rate, not equilibrium. Rule: catalyst ≠ ΔG change

Allosteric enzymes show what curve? (A) Hyperbolic (B) Sigmoidal (C) Linear (D) Exponential	B - Cooperative binding produces S-shaped curve. Rule: allosteric = sigmoidal

Hemoglobin demonstrates what binding type? (A) Competitive (B) Noncompetitive (C) Cooperative binding (D) Irreversible	C - O₂ binding to one subunit increases affinity of others. Rule: Hb = cooperative

Enzyme saturation occurs when? (A) [S] = Km (B) All active sites occupied (C) [S] = 0 (D) [E] = 0	B - High [S] → all enzymes bound → Vmax reached. Rule: saturation = all sites full

What increases Vmax? (A) More substrate (B) More enzyme (C) More inhibitor (D) Lower temperature	B - Vmax = kcat × [E]. Rule: Vmax ∝ [enzyme]

What determines Vmax? (A) [S] (B) Km (C) kcat (turnover number) (D) [Inhibitor]	C - kcat = catalytic efficiency of enzyme. Rule: Vmax = kcat × [E]

If phosphorylation increases enzyme activity, what property changed? (A) Size only (B) Charge and conformation (C) Substrate affinity only (D) Nothing	B - Adds negative charge → conformational change. Rule: phosphorylation = charge/structure change

If increasing substrate concentration does not increase reaction rate, what is limiting? (A) Substrate (B) Product (C) Vmax (enzyme concentration) (D) pH	C - Enzyme saturated; only more enzyme helps. Rule: saturated = enzyme-limited

If the same enzyme catalyzes a reaction in both directions, what is true of ΔG? (A) Large negative (B) Large positive (C) ΔG approximately zero (D) Undefined	C - Near-equilibrium reactions; enzyme catalyzes both ways. Rule: reversible = ΔG ≈ 0

What determines reaction direction when ΔG is approximately zero? (A) Enzyme concentration (B) Temperature (C) Relative substrate and product concentrations (D) pH only	C - Mass action: reaction shifts based on [S] vs [P]. Rule: ΔG≈0 → direction by [S]/[P]

Where does fatty acid synthesis occur? (A) Mitochondrial matrix (B) Cytosol (C) ER (D) Peroxisome	B - Acetyl-CoA → FA in cytoplasm. Rule: FA synthesis = cytosol

Where does the urea cycle occur? (A) Mitochondria only (B) Cytosol only (C) Mitochondria and cytosol (D) Peroxisomes	C - CPS I/OTC in mitochondria; rest in cytosol. Rule: urea cycle = both compartments

Where does gluconeogenesis occur? (A) Muscle (B) Liver & kidney (C) Brain (D) Adipose	B - Only tissues with G6Pase and PEPCK. Rule: gluconeogenesis = liver/kidney

Where is glycogen stored? (A) Adipose only (B) Brain only (C) Liver and muscle (D) Kidney only	C - Liver for blood glucose; muscle for local energy. Rule: glycogen = liver/muscle

If a protein is studied in detergent micelles, what key feature is absent? (A) Hydrophobic interactions (B) Electrochemical gradient (C) Protein structure (D) Enzymatic activity	B - No membrane → no proton gradient. Rule: no membrane = no gradient
